Local Refugee Organization Continues to Deliver Aid Six Years Into a Civil War

The Karam Foundation focuses on delivering aid to Syrian refugees, who continue to flee over their countries border and across the world, as the crisis in Syria enters its 7th year.

By Ricky Zipp

As the Syrian civil war enters its seventh year, a local organization has continued to provide “smart aid” to Syrian refugees overseas and have worked to keep people in the United States informed and focused on a crisis happening over 6,000 miles away.

Originally, Karam's work mostly supported the mission and goals of larger humanitarian organizations.

However, the foundations's CEO Lina Sergie Attar, and other co-founders, are Syrian-American and when civil war broke out in the country in 2011, they were motivated to develop their own focus on delivering aid to the people negatively affected by the war.

According to Roya Nadari, the communications director for the Karam Foundation, the organization developed programs that provide long-term support for people on the ground in Syria and refugees who fled to neighboring countries, primarily Southern Turkey.

“Our vision has been to help build futures and help people reclaim their lives,” Nadari said, describing the changing realities for the Syrian population who has been displaced since the war began. “This is no longer a temporary state. This is their life now.”

Since 2011, according to the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees, over 950,000 European asylum applications have been submitted by Syrians. Approximately 6 million people have been displaced within the country, and over 5 million have fled across Syrian borders.

The Karam Foundation focused on serving the individuals and families that make up these statistics in a very purposeful form of aid. What the foundation calls “smart aid.”

Methods traditionally used by other humanitarian organizations to deliver aid have helped, but have not led to the development of sustainable methods or programs for areas of need, said Nadari.

“Food baskets are not sustainable,” Nadari said. “They want to live with dignity. They don’t want to depend on aid that may or may not come.” In order to solve problems with resource delivery, the Karam Foundation went to the people on the ground who actually receive the aid for ideas.

Projects within the smart aid program include: education programs focused on young refugees in Southern Turkey, formula and milk delivery for mothers who cannot produce breast milk due to the traumas they have suffered during the civil war, and providing food to devastated areas within the country from local Syrian farmers, simultaneously helping those in need and boosting the local economy.

“Interest comes in waves...When there isn’t a viral image or a chemical weapons attack, I think [Syria] falls off people’s radars.”
– Roya Nadari, Communications Director, Karam Foundation

While the Karam Foundation is primarily focused on populations displaced internationally, it has recently begun developing programs for refugees in the United States due to the growing population of Syrian refugees who have entered into the country since 2011.

Syrian refugee entrances into the United States saw an increase in 2015 according to analyzed data provided by the State Department’s Refugee Processing Center.

Between fiscal years 2010 and 2013, the average number of Syrian refugee entrants into the United States was 27, never reaching a higher level than 36 in 2013.

This number began to increase dramatically after 2013.

In 2014, there were 105 entrants; in 2015, that number increased over ten times to 1,682. Syrian refugee entrants into the United States peaked at 12,587 in 2016.

For FY 2017, monthly entrants continued to replicate those of last year until President Donald Trump's Administration introduced a travel ban in February, naming Syria as one of the seven countries from where entry in to the U.S. was not permitted.

Monthly entrants fell from over 1,000 from the four previous months to below 300 in March through June and only 54 in July.

While searching for stability abroad that is not able to be obtained at home motivates whole families to leave, once they make it to the U.S., their struggles do not instantly end.

Along with finding a home, a language barrier, and navigating through complicated and tedious governmental and non-governmental replacement agencies, refugees and displaced populations do not leave by choice and there is always a longing for home that does not go away, Nadari said.

“It takes 17 years for refugees to stabilize and return to a lifestyle they once had,” Nadari said. “No matter where they are, they hope they will get to return home.”

While Syrian refugees have been displaced across the world, Illinois plays a significant role for refugees coming to the United States.

In a separate report, the RPC states, between Oct. 1, 2016 and June 30, 2017, 352 individuals from Syria have come into Illinois. Illinois is the eighth highest state in the nation for taking in Syrian refugees during this time, and the 352 individuals account for nearly 23 percent of Illinois’s total refugee entrants.

Nadari said one of the reasons for Illinois’s ranking in the nation could come from an established and growing Arab population in Chicago.

According to the Arab American Institute, Illinois’s estimated Arab-American population as of 2015 was 256,395. Nearly 80,000 Arab-Americans reside in Cook County.

Even though the refugee population continues to grow and the fighting in Syria continues, Nadari said the foundation continues to work to keep a day-to-day focus on the crisis in Syria here at home.

“Interest comes in waves,” Nadari said. “When there isn’t a viral image or a chemical weapons attack, I think [Syria] falls off people’s radars.”

Despite this lack of interest and the reintroduction of the travel ban by the Supreme Court the Karam Foundation continues to stay motivated.

“As human beings, it’s our duty to ensure that everybody has a right to live with dignity and have the same opportunities that we have,” Nadari said. “Regardless of what it says on their passport.”
